EKG Healthy Creative Economy

Healthy Creative Economy

“I dreamed of a creative country, where everything was creative, everything was creative, but not just on the surface: creative to the bone, to the marrow, creative, creative, creative, creative, creative.”

Hungary's creative industry potential is greater than what we see today.

The EKG initiative was created to provide cultural institutions, creative professionals and businesses – which are constantly being pulled here and there – with an empathetic, cooperative, rational, healthy and supportive economic and social environment, and to nurture this environment over the long term.

01

What is EKG?

The Healthy Creative Economy, or EKG, is an independent professional initiative whose purpose is to gain a clear view of the domestic (Hungarian) creative industry, to make its participants visible, to share the know‑how gained so far, and to support and ensure that doing creative work in Hungary is truly sustainable.

Creative work creates value
on the social, cultural and economic levels as well.

The European Union regards the cultural and creative sectors as important drivers of diversity, competitiveness, innovation and social inclusion.

02

Why does it matter, and why now?

On a daily basis, various advocacy bodies and groupings in the creative industries produce white papers, calls to action and independently organized data collections so that their particular field receives the attention and the cultural and economic support needed for survival, and to continue without deliberate or less deliberate interruptions.

We are starting from frustrating depths, because in the cultural and creative field – even looking only at the past century – continuity has been blocked multiple times already at the level of theory. Its actors and products have often been shamed, their achievements have been made to be forgotten or squandered. Overwriting all of this might be difficult even with full‑blown toxic positivity.

At the same time, globally unpredictable economic power relations make collective action even more justified, along with the use of our ability to rationally align different but interlinked interests.

03

Our primary objective

to create the conditions for a healthy domestic creative economy:

  • Definition of the creative field and assessment of the current situation
  • Establishing an independent professional representation of the entire creative sector
  • Presentation of economic value creation and potential for each sub-sector
  • Creating transparency in the creative industries and increasing the visibility of their participants
  • Defining short‑, medium‑ and long‑term goals
  • Developing methodologies for measuring the social and economic impact of the creative sector, and defining KPIs based on professional consensus
  • Formulating professional positions and proposals regarding support schemes
04

Who shapes the creative industry?

There are several different practices across Europe; we used the Austrian example as our starting point, because the categories analysed and published by kreativwirtschaft.at are simple, easy to understand and have appeared consistently in annual studies and reports for at least the past 15 years.

Based on this, the areas of the creative‑industry ecosystem are:

  • Architecture
  • Books and publishing
  • Art market
  • Film and photography
  • Music industry
  • Radio, television and online media
  • Advertising
  • Design
  • Software and games
  • Libraries and museums
05

Regional benchmarks

In the Central and Eastern European region, there are several well‑functioning examples of how the creative industries can be organized as a clearly measurable economic sector, and how the entire creative ecosystem can be approached and supported in a complex way.

Data from Poland, the Czech Republic and Austria support the assumption that the creative industries are an undervalued field in Hungary. (According to figures cited at the previous government's most recent creative‑industry press conference, the sector employs about 100,000 people in Hungary, whereas in Austria the corresponding figure is 214,611, in Poland 281,000 and in the Czech Republic 225,000.) It is also very likely that the sector's contribution to GDP is higher than current data suggest.
